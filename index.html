Hearo
Hearo is a conceptual wearable device, a bracelet designed to aid the deaf and hard of hearing, developed for the Republic of Korea Air Force (ROKAF) National Start-up Contest (공군창업경진대회). Unlike typical projects focused on physical prototypes, Hearo emphasized a realistic manufacturing plan and profit model due to resource limitations faced by our team of active-duty airmen. Utilizing an Arduino Nano BLE Sense with an integrated microphone, we implemented a custom sound recognition AI to identify specific sounds, like the wearer's name. Upon recognition, Hearo vibrates and indicates the direction of the sound source, addressing our personal need for clear communication during noisy activities like shooting practice. We explored various government policies and subsidies to support future development.

Despite limited access to the internet and materials, and working only during our evening free time, we focused on the core aspects of product development: AI implementation, manufacturing planning, and business strategy. This project provided entrepreneurial experience, highlighting the importance of conceptual design and sustainable business models. By focusing on the potential of Hearo and the practicalities of its production, we demonstrated the ability to innovate and address real-world challenges, even within the constraints of military service.

a CAD model of the bracelet model made in limited conditions

We wrote a business plan and presented our project to a panel of judges, receiving positive feedback on our innovative approach and potential for future development. We were placed in the top 13 teams and were invited to the award ceremony at the "The Korea Economic Daily(한국경제신문사)" Headquarters in Seoul.

Brachiating Robot
For my MECH300 class my team and I were assigned to design and build a robot that could cross a jungle gym with sporadically missing bars. It was my first time building a machine without any guidance so I immediately knew it was going to be a challenging task.

Brachiation is a form of transportation for apes, think of when Tarzan swings from branch to branch; one arm goes after the other. Thinking that this was the most efficient way of traversing a jungle gym I came up with a moving mechanism and persuaded my fellow teammates.

My team and I decided to make the body and grippers out of lightweight acrylic and Arduino with servo motors for the powertrain mechanism. I designed the powertrain and gearbox so that a minimum amount of motors and gears were used to reduce weight and prevent gear slip. Using Excel my team and I made a simple program that visualized each motor's angular displacement and grip strength.



With my knowledge of C++ and Arduino, I used a motor controller to control each motor's precise movement for each phase of brachiation. I also designed and arranged the circuitry as symmetric as possible so that there was no unnecessary shear force while the robot progressed.

Servo motor C++ code

After four months of brainstorming and building, our team was able to get the shortest crossing time out of all of the competing teams.

2023 Eoulsure PCB
For the electrical safety examination, my teammate(YDS) and I designed and fabricated custom PCB panels, most notably the RTDS (Ready to Drive Sound) circuit. This involved meticulous component selection and layout to ensure reliable signal processing from the brake sensor and activation button, making a loud audible alarm. The panels were then carefully assembled and tested, contributing directly to the vehicle's successful safety inspection.


PCB panels with its corresponding parts


TSAL panel

The photo above is the PCB for the TSAL(Tractive system active light) circuit. The TSAL circuit is a safety feature that indicates when the car is ready to drive. The circuit is connected to the motor controller and the battery management system. When the car is ready to drive the TSAL circuit lights up and the driver can start the car.


TSAL light in action

We also developed countless other PCBs through trial and error, often failing to meet the safety regulations, going back to square one. Although we tried our best and made it to the KSAE competition, undergraduates building an electric car without the help of electrical engineers and professional guidance had its limitations and we failed to pass the safety inspection. However it was rewarding seeing our hard work pay off to see our to-be sophomores eventually pass the electrical safety examination in 2024.

My introduction to Formula Racing
During my freshman year I wanted to join a club. A club where I could experience the engineering process in the flesh(and also drink beer). "Eoulsure" was the perfect fit for that mission. Eoulsure in Korean is a compound word Eoul(어울) meaning ‘getting along with’ and sure(수레, pronounced soo-re) meaning ‘wagon’ or ‘car’. As you can tell from the name, Eoulsure is a club where people interested in automobiles come together to compete in the annual Korean Student Formula Grand Prix.

We competed in the E-formula division where the car runs on lithium batteries and a motor instead of the conventional diesel engine. E-formulas are generally considered more challenging than regular formulas and bajas as there are a lot more safety regulations and systems that need to be implemented to the car. 2022 was the first year Eoulsure competed in E-formula, starting from square one with no knowledge of making an electric car.

At the start of the semester freshmen like me were not used to using heavy duty tools like grinders, welders, and drills, we were first taught how to use each tool along with safety precautions.

Me in the blue hoodie learning how to weld.
Summer break was when the actual building began, sophomores and juniors did the design process while us freshmen were stuck out in the garage underneath the scorching sun cutting pipes and pieces of metal.

Grinder sparks often burn a hole in your shirt so you need to be careful.
We went to Gunsan(군산) with our car to compete on the track. Although the process was hard(dealing with the heat was even harder) I felt fulfilled seeing my contribution on the final product. By observing from behind their shoulders I learnt a lot from my upperclassmen; I was glad that I chose engineering as my career.

camaraderie in formula racing
The moment I became a sophomore member of Eoulsure, I had a batch of freshmen that were so eager to learn but didnt know where to start. Rembering struggles as freshmen, a lot of senior members held weekly or monthly semiars to pass down what they have learned during their time in the team.

Traditionally, the team only held a power tools seminar for freshmen in the spring to prime them for heavy duty work in the summer. But as the team grew and became more diverse in its projects, we started to open more specialized seminars for different areas of interest, such as CAD, electrical circuits, racecar dynamics, etc. Additionally we invited KUME alumni working in the automobile industry such as "Hyundai", "Alpine Formula One Team", and "KAI" to give advice from an expert's point of view.

These seminars not only helped freshmen get accustomed to the technical skills required for building a racecar, but also fostered a sense of camaraderie and teamwork within. By sharing knowledge and experiences, we were able to create a supportive learning environment without any barriers between ages.

I personally assisted the Chassis team in holding weekly CAD seminars, teaching freshmen the basics of CATIA and Solidworks. By the end of the summer they were able to 3D print basic switch boxes and fasteners.
I also held my own semiar specialized in electrical ciruitry, sharing what I should've done from the start in retrospect and the hardships I've experienced along the way(2023 Grand Prix).

Sharing my knowledge and experience contributed to the collective development of our younger members, ensuring that they were well-equipped to tackle the challenges they will eventually face when making their own race car.
